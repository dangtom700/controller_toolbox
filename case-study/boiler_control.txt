Abstract: This paper details development of a Model Predictive Control (MPC) algorithm 
for a boiler-turbine unit, which is a nonlinear multiple-input multiple-output process. 
The control objective is to follow set-point changes imposed on two state (output) 
variables and to satisfy constraints imposed on three inputs and one output. In order to 
obtain a computationally efficient control scheme, the state-space model is successively 
relinearized on-line for the current operating point and used for prediction. In 
consequence, the future control policy is easily calculated from a quadratic optimization 
problem. For state estimation the extended Kalman filter is used. It is demonstrated 
that the MPC strategy based on constant linear models does not work satisfactorily 
for the boiler-turbine unit whereas the discussed algorithm with on-line successive 
model linearisation gives practically the same trajectories as the truly nonlinear MPC 
controller with nonlinear optimization repeated at each sampling instant.
Keywords: Process control; Model predictive control; Boiler-turbine unit; On-line 
successive linearization

Section 5: Test case design and evaluation methodology
Overview of test cases
Eight test cases are constructed to evaluate each controller across 4 distinct performance dimensions: large-
signal tracking with abrupt transitions, large-signal tracking with smooth transitions, regulation from exact 
equilibrium, and regulation with an initial state perturbation. Together these cases expose both the transient 
and steady-state behavior of each controller, and probe robustness to initialization errors representative of 
real-world plant start-up or load-change disturbances.
TC1: Abrupt set-point transition 
The reference signal switches instantaneously between the output equilibrium values corresponding to 
Operating Points A, B, and C at 300-second intervals over a 1500-second simulation. These step changes 
in all three output references simultaneously represent the most challenging tracking scenario: the set-point 
jumps are large (e.g., power reference jumps from 15.3 MW to 50.5 MW to 128 MW), and the transitions occur 
faster than the plant's dominant time constants. This test case primarily assesses a controller's peak tracking 
error, overshoot, and settling time under large-signal excitation. Controllers with slow integral action or fixed 
12
linear models will be most penalized here, as the operating point after each jump differs substantially from the 
design point
TC2: Smooth set-point transition 
The same waypoints as TC1 are connected by a cubic spline interpolant, producing a smooth, continuously 
differentiable reference trajectory. Unlike TC1, the spline eliminates the impulsive set-point error that occurs 
at each transition, so controllers are not penalized for a finite step-response time lag. This test case is better 
suited to revealing bandwidth limitations and steady-state tracking accuracy: a controller that cannot follow the 
slow curvature of the spline is revealing a fundamental gain or phase-margin deficiency, rather than merely 
a large transient overshoot. The ISE and ITAE scores for TC2 are therefore more indicative of a controller's 
fundamental tracking quality than those for TC1.
TC3: Regulation at fixed operating points
Three separate regulation tests are run, one for each of OPs A, B, and C. The plant is initialized at exact 
equilibrium, and the reference is held constant at the corresponding output equilibrium value for the full 
1500-second simulation. Under ideal conditions, a controller with integrating action should drive the error to 
zero within its settling time; thereafter, the ISE and ITAE accumulate only from residual numerical noise or limit 
cycling. This test case diagnoses instability, integrator windup, and poor gain margin at each operating point 
independently. A controller that performs well at OP B (its design point) but poorly at OP A or OP C is exhibiting 
gain sensitivity to operating-point displacement.
TC4: Regulation with 5% state perturbation
Each of the three regulation tests from TC3 is repeated with the initial state vector 
 scaled by 1.05, a 5% 
positive perturbation from equilibrium. This places the plant slightly outside its nominal operating region at t = 
0, mimicking a realistic scenario such as an imperfect warm-start, a load disturbance, or a sensor bias. The 
controller must first bring the plant back to the target output and then regulate it there. Comparing TC4 against 
TC3 results directly quantifies each controller's rejection capability and sensitivity to initial condition uncertainty. 
Controllers with strong integral action and good gain margin will show comparable ISE/ITAE between TC3 and 
TC4, while marginally stable or purely proportional controllers will show significant degradation.

TC1: Abrupt transition 
The output plots for TC1 immediately reveal two controllers that fail catastrophically. The lead-lag controller 
(cyan trace) diverges in both the pressure and power channels, with pressure growing unboundedly beyond 
1000 kg/cm^2 and power reaching -600 MW by the end of the simulation. This instability arises from two 
compounding factors: the absence of integral action means the lead-lag controller relies entirely on proportional 
gain for set-point tracking, and the large excursion away from Operating Point B (where the compensator 
was designed) takes the controller into a region where its fixed gain is far too high relative to the actual 
plant gain. The result is a positively reinforcing error loop that grows without bound. The LQI controller (dark 
navy trace) also shows significant pressure overshoot and slow convergence, particularly during the

The MPC advantage in regulation
MPC demonstrates the most consistent regulation performance, achieving ISE values of 828 and 1315 at 
OP B in TC3 and TC4 respectively, two to four orders of magnitude below competing controllers in the 
same test. This extraordinary advantage is attributable to three properties of the MPC formulation. First, the 
quadratic program directly minimizes a weighted squared error objective, which is exactly the ISE criterion 
being measured, so MPC is in a sense optimized for the evaluation metric. Second, the receding-horizon 
formulation allows the controller to account for the multi-step consequence of its current action, avoiding the 
integrator windup and cross-coupling degradation that afflicts the fixed-gain methods. Third, the explicit input 
and rate constraints prevent the saturating behavior that causes lead-lag and LQI to diverge or oscillate in 
off-design conditions.
The limitation of the MPC implementation here is that it uses a fixed linearization at OP B. This is precisely the 
scenario the Ławryńczuk (2017) paper argues against: a constant-model MPC is shown to be inadequate for 
large operating range excursions. The TC3 and TC4 results at OP C confirm this: MPC ISE at OP C (8.17 * 
10⁷ in TC3) is larger than at OP B by five orders of magnitude, demonstrating the model mismatch cost. The 
paper's proposed solution - successive on-line model linearization at each sampling instant - would address 
this gap and is expected to recover near-optimal performance across all operating points.

The role of integrated action
26
The lead-lag compensator result provides the clearest possible demonstration of the necessity of integral action 
for this plant. Without an integrator, there is no mechanism to eliminate steady-state error when the plant is 
operating away from the exact gain assumed at design. Given the near-integrating nature of the pressure 
and water-level states, even small gain errors accumulate over time into large steady-state offsets that a 
proportional-only controller cannot correct. All controllers with explicit integral action (PID variants, LQI, MPC 
with state feedback, feedforward+feedback) avoid this failure mode, though they differ substantially in how 
effectively they balance integration against stability margin.
Summary recommendation
Based on the combined ISE and ITAE evidence across all eight test cases, the following conclusions can be 
drawn:
1. MPC with fixed OP B model is the best overall regulator at or near the design point. Its performance 
degrades at OP C due to model mismatch, and it would benefit most from the successive online linearization 
strategy proposed in the source paper.
2. Gain-scheduled PID is the most practical classical alternative for wide-range tracking (TC1 and TC2), 
offering substantially better large-signal performance than fixed PID at modest additional complexity. Its high-
load regulation limitation should be addressed by including more operating points in the scheduling grid or 
adopting a nonlinear gain schedule.
3. Fixed PID and FuzzyPID provide a reliable and stable baseline across all test cases, making them 
appropriate for implementations where simplicity and interpretability are priorities, at the cost of sub-optimal 
performance during large operating-range transitions.
4. Lead-Lag compensation is not viable for this plant without integral augmentation and should not be 
considered further without fundamental redesign.
5. The FF+FB strategy shows promise for fast initial set-point response (lowest ITAE in TC1 among non-MPC 
methods) and would benefit from replacing the static Kff with an online-updated inverse gain to improve 
performance during operating-point transitions.
These findings collectively support the central thesis of Ławryńczuk (2017): that constant-model linear MPC 
and classical fixed-gain controllers are insufficient for nonlinear plants with wide operating ranges, and that 
successive online model linearization represents a practical and computationally tractable path to closing the 
performance gap with fully nonlinear MPC
